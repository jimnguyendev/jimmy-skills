# Outcome Thinking

This reference condenses the foundations from the management notes.

## Core model

Use this chain when reasoning about management goals:

`resources -> activities -> outputs -> outcomes -> impact`

- outputs are what the team builds or ships
- outcomes are behavior changes in users, customers, or employees
- impact is the larger business or organizational result

## Default framing

Use this template:

- impact: what business result matters?
- outcome: what human behavior would drive that result?
- output: what could we try to create that behavior?

## Magic questions

When defining an outcome, ask:

1. What user, customer, or employee behaviors drive the business result?
2. How could we get people to do more of those behaviors?
3. How will we know we are right?

## Leading vs lagging indicators

- lagging indicators tell you what already happened
- leading indicators measure behaviors that predict the result you want

Good outcome work usually relies on leading indicators.

## Hypotheses

When the causal link is uncertain, write a hypothesis:

- we believe that if behavior `X` increases, result `Y` will improve
- we will know we are right when metric `Z` changes in a defined way

Then design the smallest test that can generate evidence.

## OKR guidance

Use behavior-based key results where possible. Avoid feature-completion key results disguised as outcomes.

Better:

- `25% of new users convert to repeat users`

Worse:

- `launch onboarding redesign`

## PREP-style examples

- impact: improve retention
- outcome: learners practice 4+ times per week
- output: try streaks, reminders, or progress visibility

## Practical rule

If a manager asks for a feature, ask which behavior should change and how that behavior links to the business result.
